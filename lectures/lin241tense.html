A problem

  • Consider the following:

    (1) John is reading Doctor Zhivago.

    (2) John read Doctor Zhivago.

    (3) John will read Doctor Zhivago.

  • What are the truth conditions of (1), (2), and (3)?

    How are they distinct from each other?

    …And how are they related to each other?

Informally…

  • (1)-(3) have the same core meaning

  • They differ in tense

  • If "John read Dr Zhivago " is true at some point in time, then "John is reading Dr Zhivago" is true at some earlier point in time

  • If "John will read Dr Zhivago ."is true at some point in time, then "John is reading Dr Zhivago" is true at some later point in time

Our current system

  • Currently, we just ignore tense

  • Set theory has no tense.

  • ⟦John is reading Dr Zhivago. ⟧M,c,g,w=1
    iff ⟨J′,DrZh′⟩ ∈ V(is reading)

  • ⟦John read Doctor Zhivago. ⟧M,c,g,w= 1
    iff ⟨J′,DrZh′⟩ ∈ V(readpst)

  • ⟦John will read Doctor Zhivago. ⟧M,c,g,w= 1
    iff ⟨J′,DrZh′⟩ ∈ V(will read)

Our current system

  • is reading, readpst, and will read are distinct relations in our model.

  • We could stipulate the entailments that hold between them …

  • … But then we would have to do so for every verb.

  • We want to be able to derive the similarities and differences between tenses systematically.

A tensed sentence is separable into tense and a sentence radical

The LF of a tensed sentence

How do we interpret Stense?

  • The truth of a tensed sentence is evaluated at the time of utterance (UT)

  • The state of affairs encoded in the sentence radical is interpreted relative to UT

    • The truth of "John will read Dr Zhivago" depends the state of affairs at some point in time that comes after the time it is uttered

  • How do we encode that?

Introducing events

  • In our current system, verbs denote relations between individuals.

    • V(read) = {⟨x, y⟩ : x read y}

  • Sentence radicals, then, denote propositions.

  • Our intuitive sense is that they describe "states of affairs"

  • Semanticists call these "states of affairs" Events.

  • Events are a new type of semantic object in our model

Adding Events to our Model

  • Our current conception of our model: M = ⟨D, V⟩

  • The domain of discourse D is actually split into subdomains:

    • Domain of entities De
      {John', Dr Zhivago', …}

    • Domain of truth-values Dt
      {0,1}

  • Now we add the domain of events Dv

Events and time

  • Unlike entities and truth-values, each event has a time or interval of time associated with it

  • If John brushes his teeth in the morning and again at night,

    • … two distinct events have occurred

    • … but one individual participated in both events

  • For any given event e, time(e) is the time associated with e.

Putting events to work

  • To make our sentence radicals event descriptions we must modify our verb denotations

  • V(read) = {⟨x,y⟩ : x,y ∈ De and x read y}

  • V(read)= {⟨e,x,y⟩ : e is an event of x reading y}

  • ⟦[SJohn read Dr Zhivago. ]⟧M,c,g,w =
    {e: ⟨e, John′, DrZh′⟩ ∈ V(read)}

  • This denotation can serve as an argument to a TENSE predicate

Putting events to work

(1-3) [ST TENSE [S John read Dr Zhivago]]

  • TENSE defines a relation R between the time of utterance (UT) and the time of the event described by S (ET).

  • ⟦[STTENSE S]⟧M,c,g,w=1 iff there is an event e s.t. ⟨time(e), time(c)⟩ ∈ R and e ∈ ⟦S⟧M,c,g,w

  • Present: UT and ET are simultaneous

  • Past: ET precedes UT

  • Future: ET follows UT

Interim Summary

  • English sentences are tense specifications + sentence radicals

  • Sentence radicals are event descriptions

  • Tense specification encode relations between UT and ET

    • Present: UT and ET are simultaneous

    • Past: ET precedes UT

    • Future: ET follows UT

Absolute vs relative time

  • UT and ET are sufficient to analyse absolute time as encoded by simple tenses

  • More complex "tenses" encode relative time.

What is Topic Time?

  • UT is the time a sentence is uttered.

  • ET is the time associated with the event described.

  • TT is the time the sentence is "about."

  • Often encoded in temporal adverbials.

    • Last year, John read Dr Zhivago.

    • By yesterday, John had read Dr Zhivago.

How does TT interact with UT and ET?

  • TT mediates UT and ET

  • TT is situated relative to UT

    • "Tomorrow," e.g., situates TT in the day following UT.

    • Past tense, e.g., says TT precedes UT.

(7) * Tomorrow, John read Dr Zhivago.

How does TT interact with UT and ET?

  • ET is situated relative to TT.

  • The ET-TT relation is encoded by Aspect

  • We can assume that Aspect is encoded in all tensed sentences

What about Simple Present?

  • (10) John reads Dr Zhivago.

  • Sentences like (10) are interpreted habitually.

  • (10) roughly means John habitually reads Dr Zhivago.

  • This comes from a mismatch in the tense and aspect specification

  • Predicted: (TT = UT & ET ⊆ TT) → ET ⊆ UT

  • But: UT is atomic (i.e., not a set) so it ET can't be a subset of it

  • How it gets a habitual interpretation is an open question.

  • Progressive Aspect: ET contains TT2.

  • ET,TT2 — TT1 — UT

Summary

  • Temporal adverbials and aspect, require more than Utterance Time and Event Time.

  • They define Topic Times

  • While UT and ET are unique in a sentence, there can be multiple TTs

  • A tensed sentence relates a TT to UT and ET to a TT.

  • TT and ET are not directly related to each other, but interact logically

Not all event descriptions are the same

  • Consider the following:

    (12) John read a book

    (13) John smoked

    (14) John fell

  • Each describes a different kind of event

    • (12) describes an accomplishment

    • (13) describes an activity

    • (14) describes an achievement

Accomplishments

(12) John read a book

  • Durative: Associated with a time span, not just a point in time.

  • Telic: Has a natural endpoint.

  • Can be modified by "in an hour" and "for an hour"

  • The present progressive doesn't entail the present perfect

Activities

(13) John smoked

  • Durative: Associated with a time span, not just a point in time.

  • Atelic: Has no natural endpoint.

  • Can be modified by "for an hour" but not "in an hour"

  • The present progressive entails the present perfect

Achievements

(14) John fell

  • Punctual: Associated with just a point in time.

  • Telic: Has a natural endpoint.

  • Can't be modified by either "in an hour" or "for an hour"

  • The present progressive doesn't entail the present perfect

States

(15) John is tall

  • There is some controversy over whether states are a type event

    Or, more accurately, states and events are two types of eventualities

  • They are atelic

  • They are neither durative, nor punctual

  • Some can be modified by "for and hour"

  • None can be modified by "in an hour"

  • Cannot appear in the progressive

A final note

  • What I've described today is an incomplete fragment of the English system for encoding time.

  • There is plenty more to say about the English tense and aspect system.

  • Other languages encode time in very different ways.

  • The notions we've seen today provide a good starting point for any further study
